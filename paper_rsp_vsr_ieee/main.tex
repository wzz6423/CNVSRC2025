\begin{abstract}
Visual speech recognition (VSR) models are usually evaluated as static predictors, although deployed users, cameras, and speaking conditions vary over time. We study continual personalization of a frozen Mandarin VSR backbone when one character-level correction is available every ten utterances. We use a strictly prequential single-adapter protocol: each transcript is scored before its correction can update a 75k-parameter residual adapter. Pseudo-label updates are admitted only after CTC-based reliability checks; candidate updates then undergo post-update checks and can be atomically rolled back. On a 3,908-utterance Chinese-LiPS test stream, no-feedback online adaptation lowers the three-seed mean character error rate (CER) from 69.30\% to $67.71\pm0.18$\%. Adding sparse corrections further reduces it to $66.88\pm0.10$\%, a 0.832-point mean improvement whose direction is consistent at all three matched seeds. A separately audited expert prototype reaches $66.83\pm0.08$\%, only 0.055 percentage points better, but its known feedback-location flag could confirm growth before prediction; we therefore use it only as negative diagnostic evidence. On an A--B--C--A validation stream, target-conditioned error localization is 2.508 CER points better than randomized support but 2.367 points worse than full-sequence replay, so its pre-specified gate stops test evaluation. Corrupting 10\% of feedback characters changes CER by 0.041 points in one controlled run, while rollback and reliability ablations do not support a causal robustness claim. These results support online single-adapter adaptation and an incremental three-seed mean benefit from sparse corrections, but not the present expert or error-localized update mechanisms.
\end{abstract}
